Introduction

The Bilchick Laboratory in the Clinical Cardiac Electrophysiology Program within the Cardiovascular Medicine Department at the University of Virginia Health System has adopted an integrated approach to clinical research involving the fields of clinical cardiac electrophysiology, cardiac imaging, biomedical engineering, epidemiology, and heart failure. Our lab pursues four main areas of research, with the central goal improving outcomes for patients with, or at risk for, cardiac arrhythmias, particularly those with structural heart disease and/or heart failure.

The focus of our lab involves the use of cardiac magnetic resonance and advanced echocardiography to improve outcomes for patients with heart failure who are candidates for cardiac resynchronization therapy (CRT). We also conduct clinical and translational studies to elucidate the cardiac mechanics underlying dyssynchronous heart failure, mycardial infarction in heart failure, right ventricular dysfunction, and diffuse cardiac fibrosis. Another area of our research is aimed at discovering key genetic and molecular mechanisms associated with mechanical dyssynchrony and CRT response. And finally, the lab performs large database research to measure and evaluate the clinical benefits of heart rhythm therapies, such as implantable cardioverter defibrillators and CRT devices.

Team

Kenneth C. Bilchick, MD, MS

Dr. Bilchick is the principal investigator of the Bilchick Laboratory, associate professor of cardiovascular medicine, and clinical cardiac electrophysiologist.

He maintains the active research program in the use of cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (CMR) for patients with arrhythmias and heart failure; evaluation of advanced treatment options for atrial fibrillation; and outcomes evaluation in Medicare patients with heart rhythm disorders and heart failure. He has found that CMR -- the gold standard for evaluation of cardiac function and scar -- is very useful to helping to guide therapy for heart rhythm patients, because many of these patients have scarring in the heart or abnormal heart function. Dr. Bilchick has a grant from the National Institutes of Health aimed at using CMR to improve outcomes for patients with heart failure who qualify for CRT. He is also involved in integrating CMR technology with ablation of atrial fibrillation at UVA, developing new atrial fibrillation ablation protocols, and evaluating new drugs for atrial fibrillation. A recent publication of his discussing Medicare outcomes attracted widespread interest and is influencing practice in this area.

Clincally, Dr. Bilchick specializes in cardiac rhythm problems, including atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, supraventricular tachycardia (SVT), and ventricular tachycardia (VT). He performs a full range of procedures to help correct rapid or irregular heart rhythms, which can cause a variety of symptoms (e.g., fatigue, shortness of breath, chest pain, light-headedness, fainting). During catheter ablation procedures, areas of the heart causing rhythm abnormalities are targeted in order to restore normal heart rhythm. Dr. Bilchick also has advanced expertise in pacemakers, defibrillators (ICDs), and cardiac resynchronization therapy (CRT). During his time at UVA, he has developed a tailored pre-op and post-op protocol for patients receiving CRT to improve heart failure symptoms.

He has an MS degree from the University of Virginia in patient-oriented clinical research, and is the author of over 100 peer-reviewed publications. He continues to pursue patient-oriented research to help offer the latest technology and procedures to patients with heart rhythm disorders and heart failure.

Dr. Bilchick has lived in Spain, Latin America and the Middle East and speaks Spanish fluently. He enjoys spending time with his family, being outdoors and playing the piano.

Derek J. Bivona, PhD

Derek is the lead data scientist and biomedical engineer within the Bilchick Laboratory.

He is enthusiac about all things related to machine learning and biomechanics. In the Bilchick laboratory, he develops statistical and machine learning models to improve patient outcome in the clinical cardiac electrophysiology. Derek collaborates with interdisciplinary teams to collect clinical data and then develops the raw data from multiple modalities into well-structured data used to train his models. He has experience constructing supervised and unsupervised machine learning models such as: multivariate linear regression, gaussian mixture models, logistic regression, and random forests. Derek has employed on the web one of his published models for researchers at other institutions to use. He publishes his results in various peer-reviewed cardiology journals. Additionally, Derek is responsible for managing collaborations with industrial (Siemens Healthineers, Medtronic) and academic partners. Finally, he trains and supervises medical/engineering students in patient chart review, CMR/echocardiography analysis, ECG reading and exportation, and mathematical modeling - mentoring students is one of his favorite responsibilities in the Bilchick lab.

Derek also has research interests in utlizing data science to improve the performance of baseball players. As a former shortstop and pitcher, he is well-suited to combine his skills in machine learning with baseball. In fact, he has built an engine that gives pitcher-specific advice to improve fastball velocity based on parameters generated from motion capture data.

He has a PhD in Biomedical Engineering from the University of Virginia during which he developed biophysical and statistical models for predicting the progression and regression of cardiac growth. In fact, Dr. Bilchick was one of the members of his thesis committee. He earned his BSE in Biomedical Engineering at Tulane University in New Orleans, LA - his hometown... Roll Wave & Who Dat!

When Derek is not writing code or staring at medical images, he's either drinking iced coffee, lifting weights and doing handstands in the gym, or winning championships in flag football, ultimate frisbee & softball with his friends.
